COLLEEN D. CURRAN

Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka (1954) overturned the separate but equal judgment that Plessy v. Ferguson legalized in 1896. The Warren court’s landmark decision found that the separate but equal doctrine violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment because separate educational facilities for minority students was fundamentally unequal.

There remains some success with Brown’s landmark decision, like southern schools having the least segregated schools in the nation today. But there has been no action equal to that of the mandated integration to further diversify schools throughout the country even as demographics changed overtime. As a result, schools are more segregated today than in the 1970s and 1980s for many reasons.

Are Schools Resegregating?

The process of integrating schools in America was lengthy and at times violent. Not until the late 1980s did southern states reached almost full integration with 43.5 percent black students in predominantly white schools. Many northern cities, like Boston, MA, challenged these mandates and resisted integration for decades. The northeast today is home to the most segregated schools in the country.

Some scholars argue that schools are re-segregating as a result of legal changes throughout the 1970s and into the early 1990s. Others claim that schools are integrated, but that it’s more difficult today to maintain balanced diversification with rapidly changing demographics.





Board of Education of Oklahoma City Public Schools v. Dowell is an important turning point that helped determine today’s school segregation. In a 5-3 decision, the Rehnquist court found that district courts could permanently end desegregation injunctions previously mandated if schools could prove that they were compliant with the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The integration method of cross-district busing is also no longer mandated. The UCLA Civil Rights Project’s research shows that schools with zero to 10 percent white student admission has more than tripled following changes to mandated desegregation laws.

Today, most schools no longer reflect the racial makeup of the country. Even though research proves that better integrated schools lead to improved academic performance for all students, schools today are again isolated by race, similar to the 1950s and 1960s, and school enrollment is often a product of residential patterns. This varies when parents enroll their children in private institutions that are not located in their residential districts. Nicholas O. Stephanopoulos, professor of law at the University of Chicago argues that it is now residential makeup that primarily determines where a child will go to school and what the racial and economic makeup of that school will be.

Research proves that all students perform better when enrolled in integrated schools and that low-income students perform best at socioeconomically diverse schools. Even though city schools are better representing the population today than in years past and that white students are in more classrooms with students of color than before, some factors still separate students of color from predominantly white schools.





Shifting Demographics Without mandated integration methods, students typically attend their neighborhood schools, which, for many black and Latino students, mean schools in low-income areas with limited supplies.
Poverty and Race Poor black and Latino students, who live where a high proportion of the population is poor (living under the federal poverty line), are more likely to attend a school in that area than poor white students.
Less Opportunity Low-income schools offer less AP, IB and other gifted student courses. These schools tend to have more students of color. Moreover, there are more teachers with less than one year teaching experience at low-income schools than a white schools.
Districts According to The Center for Public Education, school segregation today typically takes place between districts, not within district. It can be challenging to integrate school systems across district lines.

What about Prince George’s County, Maryland?





School desegregation occurred in Maryland following the Brown decision. “The final decision of the Supreme Court is now the law of the land and is automatically binding upon the public school system of Maryland and upon the several local school officials throughout the State, and that the State Courts and the Federal Courts, should cases arise, are bound by the decision of the Supreme Court to recognize and give full force and effect thereto,” said Maryland’s then Attorney General Edward D. E. Rollins.

But desegregation, like in other states, was met with opponents who fought against busing as well as advocates who argued the state and school systems were dragging their feet in enforcing the constitutional mandate.

In reviewing data from Maryland local officials and school districts, it looks like the burden was placed on families of color to apply for their sons and daughters to attend white schools. Of course, desegregating school districts was a process that took time and organization. But the counties in Maryland did not fully desegregate immediately, despite the Supreme Court’s demand for “deliberate speed” in the Brown case.

The county school boards had a lot of power in determining the process of desegregation in Maryland. In Prince George’s County, school choice was subject to available spots and open capacity at most facilities with approval by the board of education. The board of education could also “delay or deny” school admission based on the community’s best interest, “public safety and community welfare.”

A United States Commission on Civil Rights in March 1976 called “A Long Day’s Journey into Light: School Segregation in Prince George’s County.” It was an investigation that chronicled the history, economic makeup and residential and population of the county; the racial population of schools between 1953 and 1964; the changes in schools from 1965 to 1972; and the commission’s conclusions. The commission staff interviewed students, teachers, parents, administrators, board members and state officials to gather the information.



What is the racial makeup of public schools in Prince George's County?

More than half of all students who attend public school in Prince George's County are Black/African American.

Source: Data collected by the Maryland State Department of Education and reported on September 30, 2017.


“Racial segregation in public education – condemned by its opponents as illegal, immoral, inefficient, and un-American – was so established in Prince George’s County, Maryland, that to many people it seemed immutable,” the report reads. The over 400-page report discusses a law suit in 1972 against the Prince George’s County Board of Education that was brought by eight black parents who argued that the county’s schools were segregated, (Vaughns v. Board of Education of Prince George’s County). The parents won, and the county was forced to create a plan for desegregation by the second semester of the 1972-1973 academic year. The number of students bused from home to school would now increase from 13,000 to 33,000.

According to a 1972 U.S., Department of Health, Education, and Welfare report, the PGC school board allowed all-black and all-white schools to operate despite the constitutional mandate to integrate. The report shows that between 1965 and 1966, “10 schools were all black, 9 were majority black, and 112 schools were 95 percent or more white.”

The mandated school desegregation distributed more materials, giving schools that were previously all-black with more materials and supplies. Myriad black students and black parents were heard by this commission, which gave them a platform to express their complaints about the school districts, when the county board of education failed to hear them before.

But one fourth of public schools in Maryland remain segregated. In 2010, the most segregated schools in Prince George’s county were almost 100 percent minority students and also had the highest amount of low-income students (over 70 percent). This is known as apartheid schools.

The population of Prince George’s county has changed overtime with an influx of Latino and Hispanic residents and less white people.

According to a Vox data set that allows you to see the attendance of public schools by race in each county, Prince George’s County Public Schools create zones not based directly on residential areas to help desegregate schools. Based on population estimates from July 2017, PGC has predominantly black residents, making up 64.6 percent of the county population.

The PGC school board divided the district into three sub-districts when court-ordered desegregation was no longer necessary as of 2002. The sub-districts did a good job representing all races and income levels. Moreover, students had the freedom to choose what school to attend within any of the sub-districts. According to the Civil Rights Project, PGC ended 33 magnet school programs as a result of budget cuts. This destroyed the county’s goal of achieving balanced school integration.
